# Recording Our Cajun French Heritage

## Why This Matters

Our family speaks Cajun French — a language UNESCO calls "severely endangered." The generation that speaks it fluently is in their 70s, 80s, and 90s. When they're gone, the language goes with them. A few recordings now can preserve their voices and our heritage forever.

**You don't need special equipment. You don't need perfect audio. You just need to press record and let them talk.**

---

## How To Record

### Equipment
- **Your phone** — that's it
- Use the **Voice Memos** app (iPhone) or **Recorder** app (Android)
- Or just take a **video** — we can pull the audio later

### Setup
- **Quiet room** — turn off the TV, radio, and AC if you can
- **Sit close** — 2-3 feet from the speaker
- **Set phone on the table** between you, or hold it casually
- **Don't hold it up to their face** — just let it sit naturally

### How Long
- **Any length helps** — even 5 minutes is valuable
- **30-60 minutes** is ideal if they're willing
- **Multiple short sessions** are fine — don't tire them out

---

## What To Talk About

Just get them talking naturally. Here are some prompts:

### In French
- "Parle-moi en français comme quand t'étais petit/petite"
- "Comment Mama faisait son gombo?"
- "Raconte-moi une histoire de quand t'étais jeune"
- Just ask them to tell you anything — a recipe, a memory, a joke

### In English (Cajun accent)
- "Tell me about growing up around here"
- "What was it like when you were little?"
- "How did y'all do things back then?"
- "Tell me about Mama and Papa"

### Great Topics That Get People Talking
- **Cooking** — "How do you make a roux?" "What's your recipe for boudin?"
- **Childhood memories** — school, church, playing outside, getting in trouble
- **Family stories** — weddings, holidays, funny things that happened
- **Work** — farming, fishing, trapping, the oil field
- **Old ways** — how they did things before modern convenience
- **Place names** — what they called different spots around town
- **Weather stories** — hurricanes, floods, hard winters
- **Music and dancing** — fais do-do, house dances, who played what
- **Animals** — pets, livestock, hunting, fishing stories
- **Church and faith** — prayers in French, hymns, traditions
- **Sayings and expressions** — "How would Grand-mère say that?"

---

## Important Rules

1. **Don't correct their French** — Their grammar, pronunciation, and word choices ARE the language. "Broken" French is authentic French.

2. **Let them code-switch** — If they mix French and English in one sentence, that's perfect. That's how Cajun French is actually spoken.

3. **Don't interrupt** — Let them ramble. The pauses, the "well...", the way they search for words — all of that is part of the speech pattern.

4. **It's okay if they repeat themselves** — Especially with memory issues. The language is in the sounds, not the content.

5. **Include everyone** — Men, women, different ages. Each person has a slightly different way of speaking.

6. **Record yourself too** — Your Cajun English accent is part of this heritage.

---

## After Recording

- **Send the files to Scott** — text, email, airdrop, whatever works
- **Label them** if you can — who's speaking, approximate date, what language
- Example: "Aunt Tina - French - cooking stories - April 2026"

Don't worry about:
- Background noise (we can filter it)
- Audio quality (phone audio is fine)
- Perfect French (there's no such thing — every speaker is valid)
- Length (short is fine, long is better)

---

## Who To Record

Anyone who speaks Cajun French, Louisiana Creole, or has a strong Cajun English accent. Priority:

- **Oldest speakers first** — their version of the language is the rarest
- **Native French speakers** — people who grew up speaking French at home
- **Mixed speakers** — people who switch between French and English
- **Anyone willing** — every voice adds to the picture

---

## What Happens With The Recordings

These recordings will be used to:

1. **Preserve the voices** — stored digitally so they're never lost
2. **Train a language model** — an AI that can speak Cajun French, keeping the accent and speech patterns alive for future generations
3. **Stay in the family** — nothing is published without permission

This is a family heritage project built by Scott Boudreaux at Elyan Labs.
